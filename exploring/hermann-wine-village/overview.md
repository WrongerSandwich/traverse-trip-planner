Hermann is a small town that punches above its weight: 
tucked into the Missouri River Valley about an hour west of St. Louis, it's a picture-book village where clock towers and steeples rise above tidy brick cottages, and when church bells toll the imagination drifts easily from the banks of the Muddy Missouri to the Rhine
. 
The land was purchased in 1837 by the German Settlement Society in Philadelphia to preserve their German traditions; the first settlers arrived in 1838 and, finding steep rocky hillsides, decided the best use was vineyards. Just 10 years later they held their first Weinfest, and by the early 1900s 66 wineries operated in and around Hermann
. Prohibition wiped that out, but the industry returned in the 1960s and today the town is the heart of Missouri's wine country — small, walkable, and unmistakably itself, with a nationally-registered historic district full of 19th-century brick buildings now occupied by tasting rooms, B&Bs, and shops.

The actual experience is exactly what the trip pitch promised: a compact downtown you can wander on foot, a handful of distinctive wineries within a short drive of each other, and views of the Missouri River from the bluffs. 
The Hermann Wine Trail is a group of family-owned wineries from quaint country farm settings to historic wine cellars, offering rich history and a variety of wine styles
. Stone Hill is the showpiece (the big tour, the hilltop views, the awards), 
Adam Puchta — established in 1855 and the oldest continuously owned and operated family farmed winery in the United States, located 2.5 miles west of Hermann off Route 100 beside Frene Creek
 — is the quieter local favorite, and Hermannhof anchors the downtown strip with its 1850s stone cellars. Beyond wine there's a serious German-food scene built around the Wurst Haus and Tin Mill Brewery, plus a couple of small museums (Deutschheim, the Historic Hermann Museum, the Caboose Museum) that genuinely add to the day rather than padding it.

The drive itself is a real part of the appeal. 
Running along the south bank of the Missouri River, Highway 100 is the most interesting route between Jefferson City and St. Louis, and midway along sits Hermann (pop. 2,336), founded by German immigrants in 1837
. From Overland Park the most efficient routing is I-70 east to a Highway 19 or Highway 100 cutoff south, but the eastward stretch on 100 — bluffs, river bottoms, vineyards — is the one to linger on. 
Exceptional twists and turns with lots of elevation changes, the town of Hermann looks to be hopping with several distilleries in the area; surprised by the railroad crossing, but it parallels the river from an earlier era
. Compared to nearby alternatives, Hermann beats Weston for depth (more wineries, more history, more food) and beats Augusta/Defiance for character (the actual 1840s village vs. modern wine-country sprawl). The honest caveat: Missouri wines are an acquired taste — sweet vidals, vignoles, the indigenous Norton — and if you're expecting California Cabernet you'll be confused. Go for the place, the cellars, and the river-valley setting; the wine is genuinely interesting on its own terms but isn't the universal draw of, say, Walla Walla.
