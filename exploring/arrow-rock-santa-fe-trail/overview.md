Arrow Rock is the kind of place that rewards travelers who actually like to walk through history rather than just look at it from across a parking lot. 
The restoration of the Huston Tavern in 1923 marked the beginning of historic preservation in the state of Missouri, and in 1963 the entire town was designated a National Historic Landmark because of its association with Westward Expansion.
 The whole village — population well under 100 — sits on bluffs above the Missouri River, and its main street is essentially a preserved 1840s outfitting town for the Santa Fe Trail. 
At Arrow Rock State Historic Site, you may wander into the historic J. Huston Tavern, which dates back to 1834 and provides a dining experience in the oldest continuously operating restaurant west of the Mississippi River.
 That's the spine of the visit: tavern, courthouse, Bingham home, stone jail, all walkable in an afternoon, with a state-run visitor center filling in the context.

What pushes Arrow Rock past "stop for an hour" into "stay the weekend" is the Lyceum Theatre. 
Established in 1961, the Lyceum Theatre produces professionally staged musicals, dramas and comedies in a 416-seat theatre that attracts professional talent from across the country to produce and perform an eight show season. Performers, designers, directors and technicians take up residence in Arrow Rock for five months to present Broadway-caliber productions.
 The 2026 season runs June through October with a Christmas show in December — 
Annie opens June 5–14, followed by 1776, then Into the Woods July 17–26
, with additional dramas through fall. Tickets are reasonable: 
$55 for musicals, $49 for plays at regular adult prices.
 The town is genuinely sleepy on non-show weekdays — 
the historic village of Arrow Rock dates back to 1829, feels sleepy during the week but comes alive on a Lyceum Theater show weekend
 — so timing the trip around a performance maximizes both the food scene and the village energy.

Versus other regional weekend candidates (Hermann, Ste. Genevieve, Weston), Arrow Rock is smaller, quieter, and more architecturally intact — it's not a wine-tourism town with a historic veneer; it's an actual preserved frontier village where the state owns and operates the buildings. The Santa Fe Trail framing also gives the drive itself purpose. 
The quickest way to get from central Missouri to Independence/Kansas City is a straight shot on Interstate 70, but the trail parallels I-70 just to the north on MO 41/65/24, a far more scenic way to go because for the most part you're driving on the original route and stopping at crossing sites, with ruts and old historic towns along the way.
 A great loop is to take I-70 east on the way out, then return via the trail-adjacent backroads through Marshall, Lexington, and Independence — actively retracing the route Becknell's wagons took in 1821. Pair this with overnights in Arrow Rock proper (one of three small B&Bs in the village) or in nearby Boonville (Hotel Frederick) or Blackwater (Iron Horse Hotel), both genuinely characterful and walkable, and you get the kind of historic-preservation, small-town, food-and-theatre weekend the trip profile is built for.
